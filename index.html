A caravan in a cable: cargo transport within neurons

Animal bodies are highly wired for information processing and communication – axons are the “cables” carrying electrical signals arising from neurons. Each axon connects the cell body of a neuron to its synapses, the latter being crucial for transmission of information a neuron to other cells. Through their synaptic connections, neurons form a complex network of cables, connecting all body parts and enabling behaviours ranging from the simplest reflex reaction to perceiving and making sense of the assortment of symbols on this very page. Axons are true bio-cables – they’re very much alive and in constant communication with both the cell body of the neuron from which they project, and the synapse where they terminate. The inside of such a cable is a whole world humming with activity. The “cable road crew” needs to transport several cargo along the entire axon for smooth flow of information along this “information highway.” The crew faces many challenges, not the least being the distance between the cell body and the synapse: some human axons are half the body length! Long-distance transport is made possible by a dedicated “trackwork system” of microtubules. Many kinds of cargo need to be moved along the crowded tracks, both in the forward direction from the neuronal cell body to the synapse, and in the backward direction from the synapse to the cell body. There are two broad classes of molecular motors to do the ferrying, kinesins for forward travel and largely dyneins for backward travel. All these “vehicles” and their “passengers” have to appropriately recognize each other, travel to the correct destination and part company upon reaching there. Moreover, many axons give off branches along the way, so yet another challenge is directing traffic at junctions in the tracks.

Our lab focuses on understanding how axonal cargo transport is regulated. We use the classic model animal C. elegans. Applying a combination of genetic, molecular and in vivo imaging approaches (see ‘Movies’), our work has revealed for the first time how certain motors are regulated at synapses. We are now working to track down novel players that regulate cargo transport in neurons. While we are engaged largely in basic research, a lot of what we do has significant implications for understanding neurodegenerative diseases in humans. Parallels between pathologies seen in transport-challenged C. elegans mutants and motor neuron disease in human patients underscore the value of research in an important area whose complexity continues to fascinate and challenge researchers.

About Lab

Our lab is interested in linking cell biology of the neuron to function and behaviour of the organism. We focus on how transport of axonal cargo is regulated, using C. elegans as a model organism. Using a combination of classical genetics, molecular & biochemical approaches and in vivo imaging techniques, our lab investigates synaptic vesicle protein trafficking, regulation of cargo-motor interactions, axoplasmic crowding and regulation of cargo distribution along the neuronal process.

Research Summary

Regulation of pre-synaptic vesicle transport.

Regulation of mitochondrial transport and distribution in neurons.

Developing new in vivo tools to help us image cargo transport in neurons.

Quantitative understanding of vesicle transport.
